Background: Quench Water Dispenser

The Data Science Initiative (DSI) at Brown University previously used a water dispenser that lacked durability and was prone to leakage. To address these issues, the staff replaced it with a new Quench water dispenser, designed to be more reliable and touch-free for improved hygiene and ease of use. The Quench dispenser offers four water optionsâ€”cold, ambient, sparkling, and hot. Instead of physical buttons, users hover their hand over sensors to toggle between selections and dispense water.

While this upgrade aimed to enhance functionality and hygiene, our study reveals that it may have introduced usability challenges for beginner and rushed users. This project examines how people interact with the new interface though interviews, personas, and a storymap documenting user experiences.

Interviews

We conducted interviews with several participants (more than three) to understand their experiences with the Quench water dispenser. Here are some key insights that capture the general patterns we noticed:

  • Approach:
    • Users began by placing their bottle on the tray, most did so without even pausing to read instructions.
    • Some took a few seconds to read the instructions and scan the available options (cold, sparkling, ambient, hot) before proceeding.
    • Users with people waiting behind them tended to act more quickly.
  • Mode Selection:
    • Most users did not need to change the setting, as cold water was most popular and set as default.
    • Some users hovered their front hand over the mode selector to toggle between the options, while others used their back hand. Some even unnecessarily touched the button directly, which would sometimes not trigger any interface response depending on the location touched and would often require multiple attempts.
  • Dispensing Process:
    • Similar to the mode selector, users interacted with the dispensing button in various ways. Some hovered their front hand, others used their back hand, and some directly touched it.
    • Some users actively watched the water as it filled their bottle, while others checked their phone while waiting, occasionally glancing at the bottle.
    • Some users misaligned their bottle at first and adjusted it so water stopped hitting the tray.
  • Exit Behavior:
    • Some users lingered briefly to wipe the spout before leaving.
    • Others left immediately after collecting their water, without any additional interaction.

We also asked each of our subjects questions to better understand their experiences with the Quench water dispenser. Here is a summary of the responses we recieved:

  1. How often do you use this dispenser? Why did you choose this one over another option?
    • Frequent users (e.g., three times a day) relied on it for convenience, especially those who worked or studied nearby. They appreciated that it was free and easily accessible.
    • Occasional users (e.g., once a week) and new users used it when visiting the DSI or simply when they got thirsty.
    • Some preferred it over other options for its faster dispensing and additional water settings (sparkling, ambient, hot).
    • A few previously used the adjacent filtered water station but switched to this dispenser for its faster flow and cold water option.
    • Others valued the touch-free hover feature, considering it more hygienic than physical button dispensers.
  2. What features did you expect to see on the interface before using it?
    • Most users expected basic water options like cold and hot.
    • Some were surprised to find a sparkling water and ambient water option.
    • Others expected clearer, step-by-step instructions.
  3. How easy or difficult was it to navigate the interface? What made it that way?
    • Frequent users found the interface easy and intuitive, likely because they had learned the hover-based interaction over time.
    • New users struggled with switching modes (e.g., from cold to ambient) due to the lack of clear instructions.
    • Many assumed the mode selector and dispense buttons were meant to be pressed, leading to confusion when pressing didnâ€™t work.
    • Some found the buttons unresponsive at times, requiring multiple hovers for selection.
  4. Was there a specific water setting you were looking for, and if so, how easy was it to find and select?
    • Most users wanted cold water, the default setting, making it easy to access.
    • Those preferring sparkling or ambient water took a few extra seconds to locate and switch settings.
    • Users who preferred hot water found the hover-based toggling inefficient, as it required multiple steps (cold â†’ sparkling â†’ ambient â†’ hot).
  5. Have you used a different water dispenser before? How does this one compare?
    • Most users had used dispensers with pressable buttons or levers rather than hover-based controls.
    • Some found this dispenser more modern and hygienic but less intuitive for first-time users.
    • Several users found mode selection cumbersome, preferring dispensers where settings could be selected directly rather than toggled.
  6. Did the dispenser meet your expectations for speed, ease of use, and hygiene? Why or why not?
    • Most users found it fast and convenient, as long as they knew how to switch modes.
    • Some liked the touch-free hover feature for hygiene.
    • Others felt the interface could be clearer to reduce initial confusion.
    • A few expressed hygiene concerns, with some wiping the spout before use.
  7. If you could change one thing about the interface, what would it be?
    • Some suggested making the buttons more responsive with less delay.
    • A few recommended clearer labels (e.g., explicitly stating "hover") and a more noticeable instruction panel.
    • Some preferred pressable buttons to make mode selection more efficient, allowing them to pick a setting in one step rather than toggling through multiple options.

Reflection

In our study of the Quench water dispenser interface, we found that while the touch-free hover controls provide a sleek, modern, and hygienic upgrade over traditional button-operated dispensers, they arenâ€™t always easy to figure out for everyone. Using empathy mapping and storyboarding, we noticed that first-time users, like "Confused Cameron," often get tripped up by the lack of clear instructions. This leads to accidentally switching modes or struggling to dispense water correctly. On the other hand, frequent users, like "Holly Hustle," have no trouble navigating the interface but feel the multi-step toggling process is clunky and time-consuming, especially when theyâ€™re in a hurry. To make the experience better for everyone, we suggest adding direct mode selections, clearer hover cues, and more responsive controls. These changes could go a long way in improving usability and satisfaction.
